History of Encryption

  • We like to think it is very new
  • Anyone like to hazard a guess how long humans have been doing this?
  • About 4000 years

Egyptian Hieroglyphs (1900 BC)

~4000 Years Ago

  • Not specifically intended for hiding information
  • More visually expressive way of story telling
  • Great at the time for illiterate people
  • Would not be fully understood until around 1820s

Roman (Caesar Cipher 50 BC)

  • Named after the Roman leader who used it in his private communications
  • Functions by shifting letters in the alphabet and substituing those letters to encode message

Greek (Steganography 440 BC)

  • Practice of hiding information inside other seemingly mundane information
  • Messages shaved into runners heads, wax on boards

Arabic (Frequency Analysis 800s)

  • One of the earliest forms of crypotgraphic analysis, used to break codes

Going Dark

  • The idea of going dark is that if everyone used strong encryption law enforcement would be left in the dark
  • Without access to unencrypted communications would be unable to protect public
  • In Reality there are many legal traditional investigative methods the continue to function
  • Warrants can still be used to compel the decryption of information when a crime has been suspected
  • Those of you who believe that 5th ammendement (right not to self incriminate) would cover this would be incorrect

Could it be stopped?

  • If it was decided, encryption is too dangerous
  • I present an interesing case study
  • 1991 Phil Zimmermann releases PGP (Pretty Good Privacy)
  • Is basically as close to military grade encryption as a civilian would ever see
  • Cryptographic Technologies are on USML (United States Munitions List)
  • Essentially treated by the government like a weapon
  • PGP was released online so technically broke Federal Arms Exportation Restrictions
  • Government Launched investigation
  • Publishing books is specifically covered under First Ammendment Rights
  • Now free to do what he likes with the book

Can't make it go away

What about a compromise?

  • Cant really get rid of it because it is an idea
  • It can be misused
  • Is there some sort of middle ground, like speed limits on cars
  • Backdoors!

Backdoors sound great, lets just do that!

  • Backdoors create intentional flaws and attempt to hide them
  • Anytime a flaw is introduced the overall security of a system is reduced
  • Given enough time, money and motivation there is no known way to guarentee an advisary will not exploit this flaw
  • Who gets to decide who has the keys? Governments dont want unfriendly governments to have this power
  • Companies dont want governments to have this power, reduces consumer confidence

Lets say it could be successfully balanced

  • For sake of argument
  • What will it stop? Human beings will always find new ways to hide things or communicate privately
  • Codes are not immediatly obvious, it can be subtle and hidden in plain sight

The Human Need for Privacy

  • Why?
  • Easiest answer morally is Autonomy
  • Autonomy is the freedom from external control or influence; independence
  • Privacy is the right to dictate how you share your information with others
  • If others can access your information they can control and influence your auctions
  • This is why privacy is considered fundamental
